Section 2: Low Contrast Text (Visual Strain)

This section intentionally uses low contrast between text and background to simulate visual fatigue and increased cognitive effort. Reading light gray text on a slightly lighter background requires significantly more mental energy and can lead to eye strain and reduced comprehension rates.

Studies have shown that poor contrast ratios can reduce reading speed by up to 30% and increase error rates in information processing tasks. This is particularly problematic for users with visual impairments or those working in suboptimal lighting conditions.

The cumulative effect of sustained exposure to low-contrast text includes headaches, decreased focus, and overall cognitive fatigue. These factors contribute to reduced productivity and increased mental load during extended reading sessions.

Section 3: Extremely Small Font (Reading Difficulty)

This paragraph uses an 11px font size with tight letter spacing and minimal line height, making it extremely difficult to read comfortably. The reduced font size forces users to squint and lean closer to the screen, increasing physical and mental strain. This type of typography is commonly found on websites trying to fit excessive amounts of information into limited space, but it significantly impairs readability and user experience.

When text is this small, the eye muscles must work harder to focus, leading to faster fatigue. Additionally, the tight spacing between letters and lines makes it easy to lose your place while reading, requiring frequent re-reading and reducing overall comprehension. This is especially problematic for users with presbyopia or other age-related vision changes.

The cognitive cost of processing small text is substantial. Research indicates that font sizes below 12px can increase reading time by 20-40% and significantly reduce retention of information. Users must expend additional mental resources just to decode the visual information, leaving less cognitive capacity for understanding and analyzing the content itself.

Furthermore, prolonged exposure to small text can cause digital eye strain, characterized by dry eyes, blurred vision, and headaches. These physical symptoms compound the cognitive load, creating a negative feedback loop that further impairs performance and user satisfaction.
